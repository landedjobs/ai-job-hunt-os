# Negotiation scripts by moment

Load this file when the user reaches the specific moment. Deliver scripts in the user's voice, adjusted to their situation; these are the skeletons.

## Moment 1: "What are your salary expectations?" (screen stage)

Do not name a number. First deflection:
> "I'd rather learn more about the role before talking numbers. Can you share the band for this position?"

(In several US states they must share a band on request.) If pushed a second time, give a range whose bottom is the real target, anchored to data:
> "Based on what similar roles at [stage] AI companies pay, I'm looking at roughly $X to $X+30k depending on the full package."

If asked for current salary, redirect to expectations; current comp is not owed to anyone.

## Moment 2: The offer call

Enthusiasm plus no commitment, ever, on the call:
> "This is exciting, thank you. I'd like a couple of days to review the full package properly. Could you send everything in writing?"

Then request the equity mechanics in writing (list below). A company that resists writing things down is generating a signal.

## Moment 3: The counter

One counter, one concrete justification, warm, closable:
> "I'm genuinely excited about this and want to make it work. Given [the market band for this level / the competing process I'm in / the scope we discussed], I was hoping for $X on base. If we can get there, I'm ready to sign."

Calibrated-question variants when they say the base is capped:
> "I understand base is fixed. What flexibility is there on equity given the gap to market?"
> "How can we close the gap with a sign-on or a written six-month review?"

Trade order when base is capped: equity → sign-on → level/title → written review timeline.

## Moment 4: Competing offers and deadlines

Name the fact, not necessarily the number:
> "I have another offer with a deadline of [date]. This role is my first choice. Can we align the timelines?"

Exploding deadline pushback (once, politely):
> "I want to make this decision well, and I can commit to an answer by [date +5-7 days]. Is that workable?"

A company that will not give one week is telling you something about how it treats people under pressure.

## Moment 5: The fear ("will they pull the offer?")

What the evidence supports: rescission cases exist and are memorable precisely because they are unusual; offers are not typically pulled for professional, warm, single-counter negotiation. Rescission risk concentrates in ultimatums, repeated re-trades after agreement, and bluffs that unravel. Negotiate once, warmly, with justification, and close.

## The startup equity question list (ask all, in writing)

1. How many shares is the grant, and how many fully diluted shares are outstanding? (Ownership % is the only number that means anything. Refusing the denominator is a red flag.)
2. What is the current strike price and the latest 409A date?
3. What was the preferred price in the last round? (Headline valuations are preferred stock; common is worth less.)
4. Vesting schedule and cliff?
5. Post-termination exercise window? (90 days is still the common default; some companies extend up to 10 years. This single term can decide whether the equity is ever exercisable.)
6. Early exercise allowed? 83(b) implications?
7. Refresh policy?
8. Has the company run tender offers or allowed secondaries, and under what restrictions? (Tenders are the main liquidity path in the current IPO drought; paper with a liquidity history is worth more than identical paper without one.)
9. Any non-standard terms: participating preferred, liquidation preference above 1x?

## Offer comparison method

Normalize each offer as: base + expected bonus + (equity annualized over 4 years, valued at your own estimate derived from preferred price, dilution assumptions, and liquidity history, never at headline valuation). Present as a table with an explicit equity-risk line per company (stage, runway estimate from company-researcher, tender history). State the assumptions; do not present false precision.
